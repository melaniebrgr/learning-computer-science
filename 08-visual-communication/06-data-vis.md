### Grids
We can escape the flatland of 2D displays by creating salience and hierarchy and making some information come forward and some come back. Tables are good when precision and exact value comparisons are useful, e.g. comparing mortgage rages when precise comparisons are required. However, are the grids "data prisons" really necessary? The lines between the data are almost as salient as the data and can often be eliminated or replaced with alternating horizontal tones of colour. There are many kinds of tabular representations, e.g. Train schedules.

### Graphs
Use graphs when the shape of data is important, e.g. the trend, increasing, decreasing. There are a number of different kinds of graphs: line graphs (useful for time series data that shows a single data changing over time. Don't link with lines if the variable changes), pie charts(better for encoding 1-2 variables and generally have low data density. Can be improved by overlaying percentages). 3D bar graphs are problematic due to perspectives issues. So, don't turn graphs into 3D unless the data is inherently three dimensional.

### Graphing guidelines
- Axis scaling can be sued to deceive by not starting y-axis as x
- Eliminate and reduce the luminance of grid lines, boxes, etc.
- If need to scale axis make it obvious
- Increase luminance, saturation, size, line thickness ex. of data and diminish non data. Peel away the non data elements
A good resource for those especially with a business orientation is, "Show me the numbers" by Stephen Few.

### Maps
There are many different types of maps. Topographical maps have isopleths, relief maps are more decorative that looks like the topography has been light by the upper left, continental maps that show all the large land masses. Inevitable distortions result from mapping a 3D sphere on a 2D plane, the land mass of Antarctica is hugely distorted, and the shape of Greenland is distorted. The Mercator projection was common for years and years: size of northern countries too large, equator displaced downward. Peters world map solves some of these problems. Also see Eckert projection. To explore further see free tool from NASA, G.Projector. Maps are often used to present geospatial data. Cartograms vary territory size by some quantifiable variable, e.g. population shown as a bit -- it gives just enough of the shape of the country to suggest its identity. Worldmapper.org can suck in or bulge out country size according to some variable.

### 3D
Objects that have height, width and depth. There are 3D depictions of a 2D screen, there's real 3D objects, there's stereoscope, and depth perception which has its own features. Be clear on what you mean by 3D. Perception of 3D space is not just visual: vestibular (balance), tactile (blind people have robust spatial perception based on touch), proprioception (the sixth sense of where our arms are in space), and depth perception (not just due to our binocular vision). Wen we see the image projected on our retinas is 2D so we have to recover 3D vision. Stereopsis is depth perception from stereoscopic vision. 5-20% of adults are stereoblind due to having lazy eye during critical development stage as a child. Other depth cues include occlusion, where one object blocks out the other object "behind it". Our perception abhors the unlikely coincidence that the shape of the object "in front" perfect matches that behind. Occlusion is the most powerful depth cue. Another cue is size. At other is decreases in saturation the farther away an object is. Linear perspective lines. Depth of field. Shape from shading can often mess us up since we a programmed that light comes from above. Texture gradients can create the perception of depth. Cast shadows show distance from the surface. There are motion depth cues, e.g. paralellax which objects further away appear to move more slowly than nearby objects.
