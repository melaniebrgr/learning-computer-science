# InfoVis

## LECTURE 4

Amanda Cox is the graphics editor for the New York Times, which does some of the best interactive info visualizations. See her interview on Spark (CBC). We get more information through vision than the other senses combined: a huge percentage of the cortex is devoted to encoding visuals. Our job is leverage the strength of the brain in finding patterns. 	

Direct perception
A field established by JJ Gibson, is a bottom up approach to design where the object should afford it use. We are not constructing meaning, but meaning is afforded. Another example is movement of the car next to you. Which makes you think that you are moving, but you're not.

Constructivism
A top down approach. The external world cannot provide affordances, but must be constructed based on prior experience. It is characterized by pattern or structure matching. Objects can be deconstructed into primitives. Vertices need to be shown at minimum to identify the object, deconstruct object into primitives that retain the vertices. Vertices allow and object to be readable and can be constructed.

Generative
Reality is somewhere in the middle. And is the interaction of a bottom-up and top-down approaches. First you build the knowledge and then apply your own prior knowledge (some of which might be misconceived).

We can only retain 5-7 chunks of information at once, and therefore chunking information into bits is a useful practise. We will especially remember it if the info has meaning to us.

We are naturally pattern seeking and see patterns even when there aren't any. From the formation of Gestalt theory came the BAUHAUS school of graphic design.

Use the acronym C.R.A.P. to remember the Gestalt principles and tie together a number of them.

We can refine our design by limiting line, colour (colour really only useful for attracting attention). Examples of refinement are the Apple and Starbucks logo. It's a process of reducing something to its bare essential elements without losing meaning.

Common errors
- Clutter and visual noise
- Using explicit structure as a crutch, e.g. "throw a box around it!"
- Not using a grid. Grid layouts make designing much easier. Typically grids are based on a system of proportion.
Colour use colour as conservatively as possible, but is can useful for the Gestalt principle of similarity, and for areas requiring attention.

Where do we start with the redesign
1. Determine how I want the information to be used
	1. Who is my target audience
	2. How will they access the information?
2. Determine how many variables are contained in the data?
3. What is the story I want to tell with my data
	1. It might be an objective view
	2. more subjective perspective
	3. Riss will inform what details I want to highlight or suppress
4. Decide on the visual treatments for each of the variables
	1. Spatial? Map
	2. Quantifiable? Chart
	3. Temporal? Flow chart or animation or interactive slider
Design AND refine
